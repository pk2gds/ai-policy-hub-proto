Buying AI

This section is for both commercial and non-commercial audiences. It provides guidance and best practice for buying AI and AI tools. You can apply this guidance when introducing AI to your solution at any point that’s covered by the terms of a contract or any extensions.

If you are a commercial professional, you should also refer to the Sourcing Playbook and the Digital, Data and Technology (DDaT) Playbook from HMG’s Commercial Playbooks series alongside your own departmental requirements.

This section supports:

  • Principle 5: You understand how to manage the AI life cycle
  • Principle 6: You use the right tool for the job
  • Principle 8: You work with commercial colleagues from the start
  • Principle 9: You have the skills and expertise needed to implement and use AI
  • Principle 10: You use these principles alongside your organisation’s policies and have the right assurance in place

Purchasing strategies

While AI is not new, the AI supply market is evolving rapidly. It’s important that you engage early with commercial colleagues to help you define the challenge that you are trying to address, to identify the right commercial solution to meet your requirements, and to discuss partners, pricing, products and services.

To ensure that you make the best decision about buying AI, you’ll need to carefully consider your purchasing strategy. This requires cross-functional teams to work together from the outset to make sure you buy the right AI in the right instances to address the right challenges. This will lead to alignment with your overall strategic goals and core business objectives.

Keep in mind that AI may not always be the best solution to meet your requirements. Other technologies, including automation or advanced analytics, may offer more effective alternatives so it’s important not to rule these out when considering AI.

If AI is an appropriate solution for you, it’s important to have a clear understanding of the type of AI you need. These include:

  • services to develop a bespoke or semi-bespoke AI solution
  • commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) software
  • new AI features in an existing enterprise application

Each of these types will have different commercial considerations.

Key commercial considerations

It’s important to be aware of and to mitigate the potential risks associated with AI when planning your procurement. These include:

  • ethical implications of AI, such as bias, privacy, and compliance with established standards. These risks should be addressed from the outset of procurement to ensure responsible use. For more information, see the sections on specifying your requirements and aligning procurement and ethics
  • social value and sustainability of your projects, which should contribute positively to society without exacerbating inequalities. For more information, see the section on social value and sustainability
  • value for money, as your investment in AI needs to deliver tangible benefits and efficiencies, and to provide a clear return on expenditure. For more information, see the governance and approvals section
  • appropriate contract terms, especially around protecting intellectual property rights (IPR), and effective contract management. These are requirements to ensure successful outcomes. For more information, see the routes to market section
  • understanding the market landscape of AI providers and technologies. For more information, see the running your procurement in an emerging market section
  • security, data and ensuring alignment with the latest legislation, regulations and policy. For more information, see NCSC’s guidelines for secure AI system development and the security section
  • innovation, which means adopting a flexible approach to procurement that fosters innovation while allowing for iterative improvements and adaptations Further information is available in the pro-bono, zero charge or trial contracts section below. You can also contact the Commercial Innovation Hub
  • previous best practice and deployments. For example, could you learn from, use, or iterate an existing deployment elsewhere in government? You should also consider opportunities to develop or buy once and deploy at scale

Early market engagement

Carrying out effective early market engagement supports a successful procurement outcome. It will also provide you with:

  • knowledge of market capabilities, as engaging early enables your procurement teams to understand the current landscape, identify existing solutions, and assess market readiness and capabilities, which will inform decisions on whether to buy, make or subscribe
  • requirement refinements, as early dialogue with suppliers can provide insight into what solutions and innovations are available
  • an opportunity to build relationships with suppliers, which encourages collaboration and communication. This can lead to improved service offerings, tailored solutions, and strong partnerships that support long-term project success
  • risk reduction by identifying potential risks, such as vendor lock-in or ethical concerns, before formal procurement begins. This proactive approach allows your teams to address these issues early and to develop mitigating strategies
  • encouraged innovation as, by engaging with a wide range of suppliers, including SMEs and startups, procurement teams can encourage innovative solutions which can enhance the diversity and creativity of AI offerings
  • value for money, as common AI models include subscription, usage, outcome and custom or enterprise pricing. By understanding the market and engaging with multiple suppliers, procurement teams can better negotiate contracts and pricing structures, and ensure both financial efficiency and competitive pricing
  • policy alignment by supporting the alignment of procurement activities with broader governmental policies and objectives, such as sustainability, social value, and innovation mandates
  • feedback gathering from potential suppliers, which will help you to refine procurement strategies and align them with best practices and industry trends

The Crown Commercial Service (CCS) offers a number of commercial agreements and tools that will help you achieve these benefits. Further information is provided in the routes to market section below.

Governance and approvals

Business case development

Depending on the scale of your ambition, you will need to create a business case for your AI project. This is an important stage in the process of AI adoption as it gives decision makers the opportunity to assess the return on investment in both resources and costs.

Before writing your business case, you should engage with a range of stakeholders and discuss your project to understand if AI needs to be used and, if so, which solutions offer the best improvements in productivity.

There are several resources you can use to write a strong business case. Consider following the Treasury’s Green Book (2022) guidance to create a fully fledged, five-part business case. This may be a requirement depending on the scale of your project and investment.

Your organisation will also likely have investment thresholds. Typically, any investment approaching £10 million will require a five-part business case. You must use the Green Book when that is the case. If the scale of investment is below this threshold, you should strongly consider using the government guidance on agile business cases.

When working on your business case, it’s mandatory to assure all non-business-as-usual spend above £100,000 for digital activities and £1 million for technology activities through your assurance boards. The process for doing so will be subject to your organisation’s requirements. Follow the Cabinet Office’s guidance on getting spend approvals.

You should always include the key commercial considerations during your business case development. These typically include ethics, contract termination, security, intellectual property, information assurance, data protection and hosting. Understanding these considerations will ensure a robust approach and successful outcomes.

Social value and sustainability

It is especially important to consider the social value and sustainability aspects of your business case, especially those that have been identified as priorities for your organisation. For example, from a societal value perspective, will you:

  • address societal challenges, such as inequality, accessibility, and public health
  • improve public services, leading to better outcomes for citizens
  • positively impact communities such as job creation or skills development
  • be accessible to all members of society, including marginalised groups, to promote fairness and equity
  • build public trust and acceptance to ensure long-term success

From a sustainability perspective, you will need to consider the:

  • environmental impact, which includes the energy consumption and carbon footprint of your solution and how you can minimise this
  • resource efficiency, to measure the extent of your resource optimisation
  • circular economy and if your solution will drive circular economy principles, such as waste reduction, reuse, and recycling
  • long-term viability of how your solution can be maintained and adapted over time, and what the impact will be on the environment

Routes to market

Consider the available routes to market and commercial agreements, and determine which one is most suitable based on your requirements. The key features of market routes include:

  • agreement scope
  • supplier pool, standards and certifications
  • call-off options, pricing mechanisms and maximum term
  • contractual terms and conditions

However you decide to source your requirement, you must comply with procurement legislation. This is primarily the Procurement Act 2023, which came into effect on 24 February 2025. However, the Public Contract Regulations 2015 will continue to apply to contracts awarded before this date and to call-off on frameworks and dynamic purchasing systems set up prior to 25 February 2025. Commercial colleagues will be able to assist you with understanding which legislation applies.

You should consider using the competitive flexible procedure under the Procurement Act 2023 to explore more agile approaches to procurement. For example, you could ask bidders to develop proof of concepts during the procurement to allow your organisation to test a minimum viable product in a sandbox before committing to contracting. This is likely to carry an investment cost that will need to be factored into the overall project / procurement.

There are various routes to market to purchase AI systems, including frameworks, Dynamic Purchasing Systems (DPS), and Dynamic Markets. Each route operates slightly differently, so you should get advice from a commercial colleague on which is the most suitable for your needs.

The CCS offers several compliant routes to market for the public sector, but there are other providers of commercial agreements that you may also wish to consider. However, if you choose an alternative to a CCS compliant route, you should ensure that it meets the terms and conditions required to adequately protect your organisation and any end-users.

An emerging market

From a commercial perspective, AI is an emerging market. There are ongoing changes in the supply base, products, services and pricing on offer. Some types of commercial agreements offer the flexibility for new suppliers to join, which often complement these dynamics for buyers.

Specifying your requirements

When buying AI products and services, you’ll need to document your requirements so that you can tell suppliers what you need. To define these needs, you should engage with subject matter experts as soon as possible.

You should start by looking at your problem statement and considering the most appropriate type of solution for your project. This might be an off-the-shelf product, an existing technology with bolt-on AI elements, an outsourced AI build, or a co-created AI solution with suppliers. The Digital, Data and Technology Playbook has guidance on commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) software licensing terms and build versus buy versus subscribe decisions.

When drafting requirements for AI, you should:

  • start with your challenge or problem statement
  • highlight your data strategy and requirements
  • focus on data quality, bias (or mitigation) and limitations
  • underline the need to understand the supplier’s AI approach, and to ensure transparency
  • consider strategies to avoid vendor lock-in and supplier over-dependency
  • apply the Data Ethics Framework principles and consider appropriate data ethics requirements and questions
  • where the duty under section 149 of the Equality Act 2010 (Public Sector Equality Duty) applies, consider what your requirements should be in terms of obtaining adequate demographic information about system training data for the purposes of assessing equality impacts prior to deployment
  • mention any integration with associated technologies or services
  • consider your ongoing support and maintenance requirements
  • consider data format, and the need to provide suppliers with dummy data where possible
  • consider the security aspects for any solution, including sovereignty requirements
  • provide guidance on budget to consider hidden costs
  • consider intellectual property rights, as any foreground IP developed as a result of using AI (such as information, data, code or software) is subject to Crown copyright and must be protected. Additionally, the risk of IP infringement and potential consequences needs to be understood, and suitable indemnifications applied
  • consider any acceptable liabilities and appetite for risk factors in mechanisms to future-proof your solution
  • consider how to measure sustainability impacts and the performance of your service or tool
  • understand that if a pilot is required, consideration should be given at the outset on whether rights are required to roll the pilot out more widely or enter into a further stage of services, as it could affect the route to market for the pilot depending on the overall value and flexibilities required.

For more information, see the CCS guide on how to write a specification.

Understand your contract needs

Before procuring AI services or software, you should carefully consider how your contract will provide protection for key technical, legal, data, ethical and security challenges, and reflect on the requirements above.

The type of AI you buy will inform your contract needs. For example, buying COTS software or enterprise applications with embedded AI will have very different contractual considerations compared to buying the development of a bespoke tool.

Be aware that some contract terms will be difficult or impossible to amend. For example, COTS AI products will usually only be available on pre-defined SaaS terms. In these instances, the SaaS terms may need to be appropriately incorporated into the relevant government template contract.

Additionally, some terms will flow down different tiers of supply. For example, an AI COTS software could use a range of different LLMs, which will have their own pre-defined terms and sovereignty.

With AI procurements, it’s important to understand the nature and extent of any foreground IP, including inventions, designs, software, databases, and other intellectual works, that are generated as a direct result of a collaborative project. Foreground IP is subject to Crown copyright, so the contract terms will need to align to this.

Review contract clauses

Having consulted key stakeholders, there are a number of contract clauses that you may want to include, which can be within an AI schedule to your contract. The list below is not exhaustive but it does highlight some key areas to help address the commercial considerations outlined above.

You should consider:

  • a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) which covers data protection requirements
  • foreground IP provisions which clearly state ownership and licensing for any IP generated as a direct result of any generative AI used
  • supplier training, data transparency, and indemnification
  • security and compliance clauses which require suppliers to have relevant security standards and to address AI-specific risks
  • audit rights which allow checks on supplier security, data practices, and compliance
  • liability and indemnity clauses, which clearly state liability for errors or security issues, and strong supplier indemnity for IP infringement
  • detailed Service Level Agreements (SLAs) with penalties
  • an exit strategy detailing data and code portability, and transition support upon contract termination

When establishing the SLAs for your contract, consider expectations for availability, performance and support, including penalties for non-compliance.

Be sure to review any supplier terms carefully. Pay particular attention to clauses on data protection, liability, IP, data usage, and security commitments.

You should encourage the suppliers you work with to sign up to the NCSC Early Warning. This system is a free service designed to inform organisations of potential cyber attacks on their network. The NCSC uses confidentially stored data gathered from monitoring activities to identify the most targeted sectors and most common vulnerabilities in order to support the government’s strategic interventions in supply chain security.

Running your procurement

Having prepared your procurement strategy, defined your requirements, selected your route to market, understood your contractual requirements and secured the necessary approvals, you can proceed with your procurement. Your commercial team will lead this and prepare all accompanying documentation.

If you’re using an existing commercial agreement such as a framework, DPS or dynamic market, you’ll conduct a call-off in accordance with the process set out in the relevant commercial agreement. Depending on the date these were established, they may comply with either the PCR15 or Procurement Act 2023 legislation. Non-framework or DPS procurements must comply with the Procurement Act 2023 legislation, relevant policies and Procurement Policy Notes (PPNs).

The CCS offers buyer guidance tailored to each of its agreements, which describe each step in detail, including completing your order contract and compiling your contract.

Using a CCS agreement will speed up the time it takes to run your procurement. For example, if you are using the AI DPS, a straightforward procurement could be completed within 20 days, although you should allow more time for novel and innovative procurements.

Pro-bono, zero charge or trial contracts

Pro bono, zero charge or trial contracts for AI products and services need to be carefully considered to ensure they do not provide an advantage to any single supplier, create an actual or perceived conflict of interest, or introduce unacceptable risk.

It is important to look at the guidance on conflicts of interest, which is outlined in part 5 of the Procurement Act. You may also find it helpful to review the Procurement Policy Note 04/21: Applying exclusions in public procurement, managing conflicts of interest and whistleblowing. This includes a guide for commercial and procurement professionals, which is applicable under the Public Contracting Regulations 2015.

Where these types of contracts are used, they should be subject to the same commercial principles and controls as other contracts, and contain:

  • clear statements defining the scope of work, quality standards and necessary performance indicators. For trial services, clear success and failure criteria must be agreed up front
  • a defined duration for the arrangement as a scope or time limit
  • key dependencies and limitations on liability
  • confirmation that any subsequent work will be strictly competed, with necessary clauses to ensure appropriate knowledge transfer from the supplier to the buyer, as well as any future suppliers of follow-on work
  • clear records, including but not limited to consideration of conflicts of interest, details of how the buyer will be protecting value for money and ensuring a level playing field for competing bids for any ongoing services.

Further guidance

This additional guidance includes key documents and further information around buying AI. This list is not exhaustive, and engaging with commercial experts early in your project remains crucial.

For further reading, consider:

  • the guidelines for AI procurement, which provides a summary of best practice when buying AI technologies in government, including preparation and planning, publication, selection, evaluation and award and contract implementation and ongoing management
  • the Digital, Data and Technology Playbook, which provides general guidance on sourcing and contracting for digital and data projects and programmes. All central government departments and their arm’s length bodies are expected to follow this on a ‘comply or explain’ basis. It includes specific guidance on AI and IPR
  • the Sourcing Playbook, which defines the commercial process as a whole and includes key policies and guidance for making sourcing decisions for the delivery of public services
  • the Rose Book: Knowledge asset management in government, which provides guidance on managing and exploiting the wider value of knowledge assets (including software, data and business processes). Knowledge assets in procurement - Annex D contains specific guidance on managing these in procurement
  • digital and technology spend controls, which detail the instances where your requirements will be subject to functional assurance of central government spending. These spend controls enable the UK government to achieve greater efficiency and better outcomes
  • Procurement Policy Note (PPN) 017: Improving transparency of AI use in procurement, which provides optional questions to help identify suppliers’ use of AI in response to government procurements and in the delivery of services to government
  • the cloud guide for the public sector, also known as the One Government Cloud Strategy, which provides guidance on commercial, technical, security, operational, people and related issues such as lock-in for those using cloud in the public sector. It aims to support the government by providing an effective way to gain benefits from using the cloud and other hosting solutions
  • "The Judge Over Your Shoulder", which provides guidance to help civil servants navigate the legal frameworks within which public bodies, particularly government, make decisions

Regulation and policy

Regulation and policy are also evolving to keep pace with the AI market. However, there are already several provisions relevant to the use of AI technologies. These include:

  • PPN 017: Improving transparency of AI use in procurement. This optional PPN provides initial steps to identify and manage the risks and opportunities related to AI across the government’s commercial activities
  • the Model for Responsible Innovation from DSIT, which is an optional tool to help public sector teams innovate responsibly with data and AI
  • a pro-innovation approach to AI regulation: government response, which details the government's response to the white paper consultation published in March 2023. This set out early steps towards establishing a regulatory regime for AI, including 5 principles to guide responsible AI innovation in all sectors
  • the portfolio of AI assurance techniques, which has been developed by the Responsible Technology Adoption Unit (RTAU), initially in collaboration with techUK. It’s useful for anybody involved in designing, developing, deploying or procuring AI-enabled systems. It shows examples of AI assurance techniques being used in the real world to support the development of trustworthy AI
  • the introduction to AI assurance, which is designed to help private sector organisations better understand how to implement AI assurance to ensure the responsible development and deployment of AI systems

Aligning procurement and ethics

It’s important to factor data ethics into your commercial approach from the outset. Guidance which can support you on this includes:

  • the Data Ethics Framework, which outlines appropriate and responsible data use in government and the wider public sector. This framework helps public servants to understand ethical considerations, address these within their projects, and to undertake responsible innovation
  • the Algorithmic Transparency Recording Standard Hub (ATRS), which is a standardised way for public sector organisations to publish information about how and why they are using algorithmic tools. The ATRS is mandatory for central government departments
  • guidelines issued by the Local Government Association (LGA), London Office of Technology and Innovation (LOTI), Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC), and Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO) on responsibility buying AI while ensuring compliance with the Public Sector Equality Duty
  • how to consider equality in policy making from the EHRC
  • the Public Sector Contract (PSC), which is a standard contract including provisions related to intellectual property rights, data protection, equality, diversity and human rights

For more information, refer to the data protection and privacy, ethics and regulation and policy sections.

Practical recommendations Engage your commercial colleagues from the outset. Understand and make use of existing guidance. Understand and make use of existing routes to market. Define your challenge or problem statement and specify clear requirements before planning your procurement carefully. Use early market engagement to help inform your requirements. Seek support from your commercial colleagues to help navigate the evolving market, and the regulatory and policy landscape. Ensure that your procurement is aligned with ethical principles and has considered sustainability impacts.
